Adaptive Intelligence: A Dense Longitudinal Neuroimaging Study of Christian Faith-Driven, Self-Directed Neuroplasticity in Parkinson’s Disease Neurodegeneration

This proposed 18-month project would use intensive longitudinal neuroimaging to examine neural changes occurring alongside the deliberate cultivation of Christian healing expectation in people with Parkinson’s disease.

Project summary

Four participants would complete repeated multimodal imaging visits during a 12-week observation period. Imaging would include diffusion tensor imaging, structural MRI, and task-based functional MRI during scriptural prayer. The project is designed as an exploratory precision-mapping study focused on within-person neural dynamics rather than population-level causal inference.

Institutional collaboration

This proposed study is being developed by The Religious Cognition Collaborative in collaboration with Yale School of Medicine.

Research question

How do brain function, structural connectivity, and corticostriatal pathways change over time as participants cultivate a sustained expectation of healing through Christian faith practice and scriptural prayer?

Proposed methods

Longitudinal precision mapping, diffusion tensor imaging, structural MRI, task-based functional MRI, connectome analysis, and corticostriatal tractography.

Proposed outputs

The project would produce participant-level functional and structural connectome data, longitudinal corticostriatal tractography maps, analysis materials, and scholarly publications. Public release would remain subject to participant consent, ethical approval, privacy protections, and applicable data-sharing requirements.

Funding status: A proposal supporting this project is currently under review. No award has been made, and project implementation remains contingent upon funding, ethical approval, facility agreements, and final protocol development.
