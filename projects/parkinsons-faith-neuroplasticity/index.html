Faith, Healing Expectation, and Neuroplasticity in Parkinson’s Disease

Can sustained religious expectation influence the brain’s adaptive dynamics in Parkinson’s disease? This proposed 18-month precision-mapping study will examine whether a structured Christian faith practice centered on healing expectation is associated with within-person changes in neural function and connectivity.

Research question

The project tests whether intentionally cultivated healing expectation can function as a form of cognitive agency: a sustained pattern of attention, interpretation, prayer, and goal-directed expectation that may be associated with measurable neuroplastic change. The study focuses on within-person neural dynamics rather than broad between-group averages.

Study design

Four Christian adults with Parkinson’s disease would complete a 12-week faith-training protocol focused on scriptural prayer and the cultivation of healing expectation. Each participant would complete repeated multimodal imaging at five longitudinal time points, allowing the project to map individual trajectories across the intervention period.

Imaging and analysis

Each assessment would include diffusion MRI tractography, structural MRI, and task-based functional MRI during scriptural prayer. Analyses would examine longitudinal changes in functional activation, structural organization, and corticostriatal pathways relevant to motor control, cognitive regulation, and behavioral agency.

Scope note

The study does not promise or presume clinical healing. It investigates the cognitive and neural effects of cultivating healing expectation as a religious practice and tests whether those effects are associated with measurable changes over time.

Planned outputs and open science

The project is designed to produce high-resolution individual connectomes, longitudinal corticostriatal tractography maps, and a documented precision-mapping workflow. De-identified data, code, and analytic materials would be shared publicly where permitted by participant consent, ethics review, privacy protections, and repository requirements.

Funding disclosure

A proposal supporting this project has been submitted to the John Templeton Foundation. Funding remains under review; no award has been made.
