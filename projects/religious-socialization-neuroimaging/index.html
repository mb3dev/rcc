Religious Socialization and Neural Organization

Does being raised religious—or remaining religious in adulthood—leave a detectable signature in brain structure or shared neural responses? This project uses the Amsterdam Open MRI Collection to test that question across structural, diffusion, and naturalistic functional MRI.

Dataset
Amsterdam Open MRI Collection, ID1000
Design
Secondary analysis of openly available data
Structural sample
N = 925
Functional sample
N = 881
Methods
Diffusion MRI, voxel-based morphometry, cortical and subcortical morphometry, naturalistic fMRI, intersubject correlation, and intersubject representational similarity analysis

Research questions

The project asks two related questions. First, are religious upbringing, current religiosity, or religious importance associated with white-matter integrity, gray-matter volume, cortical thickness, or subcortical volume? Second, are people who are more similar in religiosity also more similar in their neural responses during a shared naturalistic film stimulus?

Approach

The structural analyses examine multiple morphometric streams while controlling for demographic, cognitive, personality, anxiety, and behavioral covariates. The functional analyses test neural synchrony across canonical brain networks during movie viewing and evaluate whether similarity in religious socialization or current religious importance predicts similarity in neural response.

Current findings

Across five structural streams, no religion-related association survived correction for multiple comparisons. In the full functional sample, religious upbringing, current religiosity, and a composite religiosity gradient were not associated with neural synchrony across the seven canonical networks. Within currently religious participants, religious importance showed a directional Default-network signal that did not survive multiple-comparison correction and requires replication.

Why the null findings matter

Large, carefully controlled null results place useful limits on claims that ordinary religious socialization produces broad, stable signatures in brain structure or general neural synchrony. The findings also distinguish population-level religious identity from the more intensive practices, experiences, and commitments that may be required to produce detectable functional effects.

Outputs

The project is being developed into manuscripts reporting the structural null findings and the combined structural-functional analysis. Analysis code, model specifications, and derived results will be prepared for public release alongside publication where feasible.
