ANALYSIS COMPLETE · MANUSCRIPT IN PREPARATION

Sleep Architecture and Daily Sacred Cognition

Religious experience varies from day to day. This project examines whether the timing and organization of sleep—especially the transition into REM sleep—are associated with next-morning closeness to God within the same person.

Design
Secondary analysis of an intensive longitudinal dataset
Participants
72 adults
Observations
Approximately 700 complete nightly records per model
Methods
Ambulatory EEG, daily experience reports, and linear mixed-effects modeling
Primary measures
REM percentage, REM latency, N3-to-REM interval, and next-morning closeness to God

Research question

The analysis asks whether nights with different sleep-stage architecture are followed by measurable changes in daily sacred cognition. It replicates an earlier association between REM quantity and next-morning closeness to God and extends the analysis to the timing of the first REM period and its relationship to preceding slow-wave sleep.

Current findings

Within-person REM percentage reproduced the direction and magnitude of the earlier finding, although it did not survive correction across the full sleep-architecture screen. Two timing measures did survive false-discovery-rate correction: on nights when participants entered REM later than their own average, or when the interval between initial N3 sleep and first REM was wider than usual, they reported lower closeness to God the following morning.

Interpretation

The findings suggest that REM timing may be associated with next-morning sacred cognition alongside REM quantity. They do not establish causation, and the timing measures are highly correlated, so they should be interpreted as convergent indicators of sleep-cycle organization rather than separate mechanisms.

Limitations

The study is correlational, closeness to God was measured with a single daily item, and one timing analysis excluded nights without detectable N3 sleep. The findings should therefore be treated as a robust secondary-analysis signal that motivates replication and experimental work, not as evidence that a particular sleep pattern causes religious experience.

Outputs

A manuscript reporting the replication, timing analyses, sensitivity checks, and limitations is in preparation. The project demonstrates the value of validated ambulatory EEG for studying religious cognition in everyday settings.
