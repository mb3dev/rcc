Sleep Architecture and Daily Sacred Cognition

Religious experience fluctuates from day to day. This project tests whether the timing and organization of the sleep cycle—especially how long the brain takes to reach REM sleep—are associated with next-morning closeness to God within the same person, beyond the amount of REM sleep alone.

Design
Design
Secondary analysis of a two-week intensive longitudinal EEG diary study
Participants
72 adults
Observations
Approximately 700 complete nightly records per model
Methods
Validated ambulatory EEG, daily experience reports, and linear mixed-effects models
Primary measures
REM percentage, REM latency, the N3-to-REM interval, and next-morning closeness to God

Research question

An earlier study found that within-person increases in REM-sleep quantity were followed by higher next-morning closeness to God. This project replicates that association and asks a more specific question: does the timing of REM sleep—how quickly REM is reached, and how it is situated relative to preceding slow-wave sleep—also relate to next-morning sacred cognition?

Current findings

Within-person REM percentage reproduced the earlier association, though it did not survive correction across the full sleep-architecture screen. Two timing measures did survive false-discovery-rate correction: on nights when participants entered REM later than their own average, or when the interval between first deep (N3) sleep and first REM was wider than usual, they reported lower closeness to God the following morning. Both effects held in leave-one-out and high-quality-recording sensitivity analyses.

Interpretation

REM latency and the N3-to-REM interval are highly correlated and are best read as two convergent indicators of sleep-cycle timing rather than separate mechanisms. The design is correlational and cannot establish direction: sleep timing may condition next-morning experience, prior states may alter sleep, or both may reflect shared influences.

Limitations

The analytic sample of 72 persons is suited to medium rather than small within-person effects; closeness to God was measured with a single daily item; and the N3-to-REM interval could not be computed on nights without detectable deep sleep, so that result applies to nights with identifiable N3 sleep. The findings motivate replication and experimental work rather than causal claims.

Outputs

A manuscript reporting the replication, the timing analyses, sensitivity checks, and limitations is in preparation. The project demonstrates the value of validated ambulatory EEG for studying religious cognition in everyday settings.
