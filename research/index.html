Research

Research

RCC investigates religion as a cognitive, psychological, neural, social, and cultural phenomenon. Our work is organized around research questions rather than a single method or theoretical school. Projects may be empirical, computational, qualitative, or conceptual, depending on the problem being studied.

Research areas

Cognitive Science of Religion

We study the cognitive processes involved in religious concepts, beliefs, practices, and forms of reasoning, including how people represent supernatural agents, sacred objects, ritual efficacy, divine action, moral order, and religious identity.

Cognitive Neuroscience of Religion

We examine how distributed cognitive and neural systems contribute to religious belief, experience, practice, and social cognition, including neuroimaging, behavioral neuroscience, and the critical reanalysis of existing neuroscience claims.

Religious Experience and Consciousness

We investigate experiences interpreted as religious, spiritual, mystical, revelatory, or supernatural, considering their phenomenology, cognitive structure, psychological context, interpretation, and consequences.

Dreams and Religious Cognition

We study how dreams may generate, simulate, organize, or transform religious concepts and experiences, and the relationship between dream content and waking cognition.

Belief Formation, Development, and Transmission

We examine how religious beliefs and concepts are acquired, reinforced, revised, communicated, and transmitted across development, social learning, narrative, ritual, and cultural context.

Methods and Theory

We support methodological and theoretical work that strengthens the study of religious cognition, from behavioral experiments and psychometrics to computational analysis, qualitative research, and conceptual modeling.

Research principles

Questions before methods

Projects begin with a clear question and use the methods best suited to answer it.

Multiple levels of explanation

We distinguish cognitive, psychological, neural, social, and cultural explanations while examining how they interact.

Claims proportionate to evidence

We avoid both reductionism and overinterpretation. Findings should support conclusions, not merely decorate them.

Religious diversity

Research questions and samples should not treat one religious tradition as the default model of religion when broader comparison is possible.
